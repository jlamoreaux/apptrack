In 2025 I decided I was done. Not fired, not laid off. Just done. After years at the same company I finally committed to a serious job search. Within a few weeks I had 40 open applications across 15 companies.

My spreadsheet had columns for company, role, date applied, status, recruiter name, salary range, and notes. By week three I had a second tab for interview prep. By week four the whole thing was a mess I dreaded opening.

Sound familiar?

## What you actually need to track

Most job search advice tells you to track the basics: company, role, status. That's a start, but it misses the stuff that actually matters during an active search.

**The status history.** Not just where an application is now, but how it got there. "Applied March 1, recruiter screen March 8, technical interview March 15." A spreadsheet shows you a cell. You need a timeline.

**The follow-up queue.** You applied two weeks ago and haven't heard back. Do you follow up? Spreadsheets don't remind you. They just sit there.

**The interview prep.** When you finally get to the interview stage, you need the job description, your notes, and your tailored resume in one place. Not in three tabs and a Google Doc.

**The version of your resume you used.** Three weeks into a search, do you remember which resume you sent to each company? Neither do I.

## Why spreadsheets break down

Spreadsheets work fine for the first 10 applications. Then you start applying to more roles and the cracks show.

Filtering by status is clunky. You can't attach files. There's no reminder system. And every time you want a simple answer like "how many companies am I waiting to hear back from," you're doing manual math.

The bigger problem: spreadsheets require discipline to maintain. When you're in the middle of a job search, discipline is in short supply. You're exhausted, you're anxious, and updating a spreadsheet is the last thing you want to do.
