In 2025 I started a real job search for the first time in years. Within a few weeks I had 40 open applications and a spreadsheet with columns for company, role, date applied, status, recruiter name, salary range, and notes.

By week three I had a second tab for interview prep. By week four I was staring at the thing trying to remember which rows mattered and which were dead. The spreadsheet just wasn't built for this.

I wanted to open one screen and know: what's active, what needs follow-up, what's dead. Not scan rows. Not filter and re-sort and squint.

## What you actually need to track

Most job search advice tells you to track company, role, and status. That's a start, but it misses the stuff you actually scramble for mid-search.

**The status history.** Not just where an application is now, but how it got there. "Applied March 1, recruiter screen March 8, technical interview March 15." A spreadsheet gives you a cell. What you want is a timeline.

**The follow-up queue.** You applied two weeks ago and haven't heard back. Should you follow up? When did you last reach out? Spreadsheets don't track this. They just sit there.

**Interview prep and the resume you sent.** When you get to the interview stage you need the job description, your notes, and the specific resume you submitted. Not scattered across three tabs, a Google Doc, and your downloads folder. Three weeks in, you won't remember which version you sent where. I didn't.

## Why spreadsheets stop working

Spreadsheets are fine at 10 applications. At 30 or 40, you're just staring at rows.

You open the sheet and your eyes glaze over. Which ones are still active? Which ones have gone quiet? You have to read every row, check dates, mentally reconstruct where each one stands. There's no shape to the data — it's all flat. A company you interviewed with last week looks the same as one that ghosted you a month ago.

You can sort and filter, but that only answers one question at a time, and you have to already know what you're looking for. It doesn't help with the application you forgot to follow up on, or the interview you didn't prep for because it was buried in row 34.

After a while you stop opening the sheet entirely. Not because you're lazy, but because it takes more effort to parse than it gives back.

## What I built instead

I started building AppTrack because I wanted something I'd actually open. Not another table. Something where I could see all my applications at once, sorted by where they actually stood. If something needed attention it was obvious. If something had gone cold I didn't have to figure that out by checking dates.

Each application keeps its own history. Every status change, every note, the resume I used. When I had an interview coming up I could pull up that one application and have the full picture right there.

I added cover letter generation because I was spending 30 minutes per application writing them from scratch. Now I paste the job description, get a draft, and edit from there. You still have to rewrite parts of it, but you're editing, not starting from zero.

I also built a dashboard. It tells me how many applications are active, how many interviews I have this week, how many things have gone quiet. Stuff I used to count by scrolling through the sheet.
